A useful pet emergency plan answers four questions before you are under pressure: where your pets can go, who can help if you are away, what supplies need to move with them, and where someone can find their current records. You do not need to solve every possible disaster today. Start with a plan your household can follow, then review it when contact details, medications, or care needs change.

## Start your pet emergency plan with two decisions

First, decide where you could take each pet if you had to leave home. The [CDC notes that many disaster evacuation centers do not accept pets](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-pets/emergency-preparedness/index.html), so identify more than one realistic option. That might include a pet-friendly hotel, a boarding facility outside your immediate area, or a friend or relative who has agreed to help.

Write down the address, phone number, any species or size restrictions, and what records the location may request. If you have several pets, confirm that the option can take all of them. A plan that works for one small dog may not work for three cats, a large dog, or an animal with specialized housing needs.

Second, choose a backup person. The CDC recommends a buddy system with a trusted neighbor, friend, or relative who can check on or evacuate pets when you are not home. Give that person clear permission, a way to enter your home, carrier or leash locations, and a copy of the information they would need to act.

## Build a pet emergency plan around current information

In a rushed handoff, a caregiver needs a short, reliable summary rather than every document you own. Prepare one page or digital profile for each pet with:

- Your name and current contact information
- A backup caregiver and out-of-area contact
- Your regular veterinarian and a nearby emergency clinic
- A recent photo and basic identifying details
- Microchip number and registration company
- Current medications and the instructions supplied by your veterinarian
- Allergies or other critical notes already documented for the pet
- Vaccination records and relevant medical documents

It does not decide what medical information is important or replace instructions from your veterinarian. If a medication, allergy, or care detail is unclear, confirm it with the veterinarian who manages your pet's care.

Keep a paper copy with your physical emergency supplies in case your phone is unavailable or has no power. Mark the date you last reviewed the information so a helper can tell whether it may be outdated.

## Prepare supplies for sheltering or evacuation

Records are only one part of the plan. The CDC’s [pet disaster kit checklist](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-pets/media/pdfs/disaster-prep-Pet-Emergency-Checklist-1.pdf) includes veterinary documents, food, water, medications, feeding and medication instructions, identification, carriers or leashes, sanitation supplies, and recent photos.

Build the kit around the pets you actually have. Useful basics include:

- Food and water in containers you can move
- Bowls and a manual can opener if needed
- Medications in their labeled containers
- Written instructions provided by your veterinarian
- A leash, harness, carrier, or other safe transport equipment
- ID tags and current microchip information
- Cleanup supplies, litter, or bedding appropriate for the animal
- Copies of vaccination, prescription, ownership, and medical records
- A recent photo of you with each pet

Check expiration dates and replace used supplies during a regular household task, such as testing smoke alarms or reviewing insurance documents. If your veterinarian changes a prescription or care instruction, update both the physical kit and the saved record.

## Make the plan workable for every pet

A multi-pet plan needs assignments. Decide who takes each carrier, who grabs the supply bag, and who checks hiding places. Label carriers with the pet's name and your contact information. Keep leashes and carriers somewhere accessible rather than behind seasonal storage.

[Ready.gov advises pet owners to plan evacuation destinations, develop a buddy system, keep vaccination copies, and maintain up-to-date identification](https://www.ready.gov/sites/default/files/2023-06/ready.gov_prepare-pets-for-disasters.pdf). It also recommends practicing ahead of time. A short practice can reveal practical problems, such as a carrier that no longer fits, a cat that hides when the alarm sounds, or one person being unable to move all pets safely.

If a pet has mobility needs, temperature-sensitive medication, or equipment that requires power, ask your veterinarian what should be included in that animal's plan. PawProof can store the resulting documents and dates, but it should not be used to invent emergency medical instructions.

## Practice the pet emergency plan and keep it current

Walk through the plan without creating a frightening situation for your pets. Confirm that household members know where carriers, leashes, supplies, and records are kept. Test whether the backup caregiver can access the home and find the correct pet profiles.

Review the plan when you move, change veterinarians, add a pet, change a medication, or choose a new backup caregiver. Also confirm that phone numbers, microchip registrations, and evacuation options are still current.

For routine handoffs, a [pet sitter records checklist](/pet-sitter-records-checklist) can help you separate everyday instructions from disaster planning. For a fuller care history, keep your [pet medical records](/pet-medical-records) current so the documents are ready before you need to assemble an emergency packet.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the most important part of a pet emergency plan?

Start with a safe destination and a backup person who can help when you are away. Supplies and records matter, but the plan also needs a specific person who knows where the pets are and where they can go.

### Should a pet emergency plan include medical records?

Include current vaccination records, medication instructions, relevant medical summaries, veterinarian contacts, and other documents a caregiver may need. Ask your veterinarian which records are appropriate for your pet's circumstances.

### Do I need both digital and paper copies?

Keeping both is practical. Digital records are easier to update and share, while a paper copy remains available if your phone has no power, service, or internet access.

### How often should I update the plan?

Review it whenever contact information, medications, veterinarians, evacuation options, or household pets change. A regular scheduled review also helps catch expired supplies and outdated documents.
